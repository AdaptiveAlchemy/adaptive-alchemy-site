faq:
  - question: 'Why do most enterprise AI projects fail?'
    answer: 'Most enterprise AI projects fail due to one of five root causes. Poor use case selection: the problem chosen does not have sufficient data, business impact was never defined precisely enough to measure, or the problem was not a good fit for AI methods. The POC-to-production gap: a proof of concept demonstrates model performance on sample data but does not constitute a production system — production requires data pipelines, monitoring, drift detection, fallback mechanisms, and governance controls that most POC builds never include. Organizational misalignment: the AI team is disconnected from the business operations they are supposed to improve, creating science projects rather than production deployments. Governance absence: without clear ownership, monitoring, and accountability structures, AI systems degrade in production without anyone noticing until it becomes a business problem. Vendor dependency: when a vendor delivers a model but the internal team has no capability to maintain, monitor, or improve it, the system becomes a liability rather than an asset. Each of these is fixable, but only after it is correctly identified as the actual root cause.'
  - question: 'How do you rescue a stalled AI project?'
    answer: 'We start with a root cause diagnosis that identifies the actual failure point — whether data quality, use case selection, engineering execution, organizational alignment, or vendor delivery. Then we revalidate the use case, assess what technical work can be salvaged, and build a phased restart plan with quick wins in the first 30 to 60 days to rebuild internal confidence and demonstrate progress.'
  - question: 'What is the difference between an AI proof of concept and production AI?'
    answer: 'An AI proof of concept demonstrates that a model can produce acceptable results on sample data. Production AI requires data pipelines, monitoring, drift detection, fallback mechanisms, governance controls, integration with business workflows, and operational processes for ongoing maintenance. The gap between POC and production is where most AI initiatives stall. Closing that gap requires engineering discipline, not data science alone.'
  - question: 'How long does it take to get a stalled AI project back on track?'
    answer: 'A typical rescue engagement begins with a 2-to-3-week diagnostic phase that produces a clear root cause analysis and restart plan. Quick wins are delivered within 30 to 60 days to rebuild stakeholder confidence. Full production deployment depends on the complexity of the use case, but most initiatives can reach production within 3 to 6 months from restart with the right approach and commitment.'
  - question: 'Should we restart our AI project or try a different use case?'
    answer: 'That depends on the root cause of the stall. If the original use case was viable but the approach was flawed, a restart with corrected methodology is often faster and cheaper than starting over. If the use case itself was poorly selected — meaning the data was insufficient, the business impact was unclear, or the problem was not suitable for AI — then pivoting to a better-qualified use case is the right move. Our diagnostic process makes this determination early.'
  - question: 'How do you decide whether to restart a failed AI project or abandon it and start with a different use case?'
    answer: 'The decision comes down to root cause, not sunk cost. If the original use case was viable — meaning the business problem was clear, the data was sufficient, and the expected value was real — but the approach was flawed, a restart with corrected methodology is usually faster and cheaper than starting over with a new use case. The most common fixable failures are: poor architecture choices, inadequate data pipelines, insufficient production readiness work, and misaligned team structure. If the root cause is instead a poorly selected use case — the problem was not a good fit for AI, the data was insufficient, or the business impact was never specific enough to measure — then restarting the same use case compounds the original error. A diagnosis-first engagement answers this question in the first two to three weeks, before any new commitment is made.'
